Why is everything so normal?

Most people have heard of the normal distribution, the pdf of this distribution is a Gaussian function, specified by two parameters μ\muμ and σ\sigmaσ:

an identity which is worth memorizing. Even in times of the internet. The function is bell-shaped with a peak at μ\muμ and an approximate width of σ\sigmaσ.

Now it’s true that this pdf appears everywhere in science. But why? Why not for example the Cauchy distribution that looks like this

p(x;μ,σ)=1π(σσ2+(x−μ)2)p(x;\mu,\sigma)=\frac{1}{\pi}\left(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma^{2}+(x-\mu) ^{2}}\right)p(x;μ,σ)=π1​(σ2+(x−μ)2σ​)

This is also a Bell-shaped curve with a maximum at μ\muμ and and a width that seems to be proportional to σ\sigmaσ. It turns out that this pdf doesn’t have a mean or a variance. But nevertheless, that can’t be the reason why the Gaussian normal distribution is so widespread.

Now if we interpret the individual XnX_{n}Xn​ as random steps on the x−x-x−axis drawn from the pdf p(x)p(x)p(x) then YYY is the position after NNN steps. The quantity

The central limit theorem

There’s more: We can define a new variable

ZN=YN/σNZ_{N}=Y_{N}/\sigma\sqrt{N}ZN​=YN​/σN​

which is just the position devided by the scaling factor σN\sigma\sqrt{N}σN​. Obviously the pdf for this random variable should depend on the specific functional properties of the pdf p(x)p(x)p(x) of the single steps. However, as we increase the step number NNN the pdf for ZZZ approaches a Gaussian and looks like this

p(z)=12πe−z2/2.p(z)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-z ^{2}/2}.p(z)=2π​1​e−z2/2.

This is called the central limit theorem. Sloppily, we can say the sum of independent identically distributed random variables will be distributed like a Gaussian. So for the original YNY_{N}YN​ variable this implies

p(y)=12πσ2Ne−y2/2σ2Np(y)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma ^{2} N}}e ^{ -y ^{2}/2\sigma ^{2} N}p(y)=2πσ2N​1​e−y2/2σ2N

The central limit theorem is the reason why the Gaussian distribution is so abundant. Whenever we have increments that are independent or random forces that impact a system in one way or another, we can expect the outcome variable to be normally distributed. In a sense all the information and the structure in the statistics of the single steps, so all the functional characteristics in p(x)p(x)p(x) are washed away. Again, this is also true in any dimension. Even if two different random walks, each defined by its own single step pdf, look initially very different, as NNN increases the trajectories of the walks not only appear to look very similar but the statistics of the position always approaches a Gaussian distribution, see the panel below for a geometric interpretation of the central limit theorem in two dimensions.

Stochastic differential equations

The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process

Now let’s look at the 1D dynamical system

x˙=−kx=f(x)\dot{x}=-kx=f(x)x˙=−kx=f(x)

This is pretty much the simplest dynamical system we can think of. Physically this might describe a mass on a spring immersed in a viscous liquid like oil that yields an overdamped movement. It will always equilibrate to the x=0x=0x=0 stationary state. Let’s aufdrösel this again

x(t+Δt)=x(t)−Δt×k,x(t)x(t+\Delta t)=x(t)-\Delta t\times k\\,x(t)x(t+Δt)=x(t)−Δt×k,x(t)

So in the small time interval the location changes by −Δt×k,x(t)-\Delta t\times k\\,x(t)−Δt×k,x(t). Now let’s assume that in this time interval the mass is exposed to millions, billions and trillions of elastic collisions with small molecules with small mass that transfer energy and momentum to the object and change the position randomly by very very tiny increments ±δ\pm\delta±δ. Because of what we said above the pdf for the displacement ΔW\Delta WΔW is distributed like a Gaussian with a certain width and zero mean. The typical size of that displacement is given by

⟨(ΔW)2⟩=σ2Δt\left\langle \left(\Delta W\right)^{2}\right\rangle =\sigma^{2}\Delta t⟨(ΔW)2⟩=σ2Δt

because Δt\Delta tΔt is the time interval that accumulates all the zillions of little kicks. So we think of the time evolution as

x(t+Δt)=x(t)−Δt×k,x(t)+σΔWx(t+\Delta t)=x(t)-\Delta t\times k\\,x(t)+\sigma\Delta Wx(t+Δt)=x(t)−Δt×k,x(t)+σΔW

where the ΔW\Delta WΔW is drawn from a normal pdf with a variance of Δt\Delta tΔt:

p(w)=12πΔte−w2/2Δtp(w)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\Delta t}}e^{-w ^{2}/2\Delta t}p(w)=2πΔt​1​e−w2/2Δt

Now we can make Δt\Delta tΔt as small as we want as long as we guarantee that in this small time interval there are very many small kicks happening. Then we get

dX=−kXdt+σdWdX=-kXdt+\sigma dWdX=−kXdt+σdW

Note that we avoid deviding by dtdtdt. Some people do that. We don’t. The reason for this is that it makes sense to talk about dWdWdW but not about dW/dtdW/dtdW/dt.

The process above is known as the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. You can explore it in panel above. And the equation above is called a stochastic differential equation specifically a Langevin-equation.

In the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, the position variable is driven towards the origin by the linear force term but wiggles around that stable stationary state, driven by the stochastic noise. The larger that noise strength σ\sigmaσ the more wiggle, the stronger the spring constant the smaller the wiggle.

Random Events - Poisson process

Quite often, in dynamical processes we have a situation in which as time progresses random events occur. For example, let’s say time is at t=t0t=t_{0}t=t0​ and it advances to a later time t0+Δtt_{0}+\Delta tt0​+Δt where Δt\Delta tΔt is as small as we like. Now let’s assume that there’s a small probability that in this time interval [t0,t0+Δt]\left[t_{0},t_{0}+\Delta t\right][t0​,t0​+Δt] and event occurs, e.g. a collision of two particles, the birth of an animal, the death of an animal or the firing of neuronal spike. Let’s call this probability

p=αΔtp=\alpha\Delta tp=αΔt

The proportionality constant α\alphaα is called a probability rate. Now let’s assume that every time we advance time by Δt\Delta tΔt an event can occur. Let’s ask: What is the probability density that an event doesn’t occur for a time TTT and occurs exactly in the time intervale [t0,t0+t+Δt]\left[t_{0},t_{0}+t+\Delta t\right][t0​,t0​+t+Δt]. We can split the time-interval into NNN small segments of duration Δt\Delta tΔt so NΔt=tN\Delta t=tNΔt=t. The probability that no event occurs is

(1−p)N\left(1-p\right)^{N}(1−p)N

multiplied by the probability that the event occurs in [t0,t0+t+Δt]\left[t_{0},t_{0}+t+\Delta t\right][t0​,t0​+t+Δt] which is ppp so

(1−p)Np=(1−αΔt)NαΔt\left(1-p\right)^{N}p=\left(1-\alpha\Delta t\right)^{N}\alpha\Delta t(1−p)Np=(1−αΔt)NαΔt =(1−αt/N)NαΔt=\left(1-\alpha t/N\right)^{N}\alpha\Delta t=(1−αt/N)NαΔt

In the limit N→∞N\rightarrow\inftyN→∞ this becomes

p(t)dt=αe−αtdtp(t)dt=\alpha e^{-\alpha t}dtp(t)dt=αe−αtdt

So the probability density for the time-interval between events is an exponential pdf. This process is known as the Poisson process.
